## Slide 7-8: Brand Identity — "Those are not brands"

Okay, chances are the logos you drew on this page... plot twist, those are not brands. The reason I asked you to draw logos from memory is first because a good logo should be able to be drawn by anyone very quickly. So you know that's a good logo. Because it's memorable, easy to recognize, scalable. You can put it very small or very big, you still recognize it.

But the main reason I'm telling you to do that is because there's a big misconception in the world of branding and marketing that those little things that you draw are brands, and they are not. They are just signatures, they are logos, they are a connection that you make between this piece of drawing and the actual company. Those are part of what is called the brand identity.

The brand identity is actually very tangible. It's all the tangible assets that the company has to communicate its identity. It's all the colors, the font, the shapes, the signatures. Those are very powerful. You can create recognition and a feeling and really attract the right user just by using a powerful brand identity. And again, those are just pieces of drawing.

## Slide 17: The Bubble Analogy

I came up with an analogy that I like using: the analogy of a bubble. In the middle appears your product or service.

What does that mean? Anyone... your clients, partners, anyone around you that you want them to discover anything about your product or even buy your product eventually. If you want anyone to be exposed to your product or service and potentially buy from you, they need to poke inside the bubble. There is no way to see the product without seeing the brand.

It's your job as an entrepreneur to find a way to attract people inside the bubble. If from the outside there is no attraction, and the attraction starts very much at the first impression... if no one is interested in poking inside, you will never sell your product as good as it can be. You cannot lead with just quality product.

## Slide 19: Jessica Walsh Quote — "If no one hates it, no one really loves it"

We talked about how attraction is important to attract people inside your bubble. But attraction is very subjective too. And the thing about subjective things is that some people like it and some people don't.

The problem with a lot of people in branding and marketing is they really want to attract everyone. It is impossible. You will never be able to attract everyone. There's no brand that everybody likes, even Apple, which is one of the most successful brands in the world. Some people hate it. But that's okay because if no one hates it, no one really loves it.

So it's okay to actually try to not attract some people. One of the exercises I do in my workshop is to help you find who are the people who are not going to own your product even if you pay them. You need to define those people to help you sometimes define who is the right target.

## Slide 20: Brain Power — 90% Emotional / 10% Rational

Now we're going to talk about biology and science because even though emotions are intangible, they still start in the brain. Our brain is made of neurons. Different parts of the brain control different things, like language and rational thinking.

The reason it's so important to use emotion in branding is because 90% of the brain power is used by the emotions and the unconscious. We often hear "we only use 10% of the brain." Well, that's not really true. That's the prefrontal cortex, which is the rational thinking.

The unconscious is the pilot's seat of your brain that makes decisions on the go. That's what you feel conscious of. But a lot of things happen in the background. It would be impossible for us to relearn how to talk, how to walk every single time. Our brain filters all the information we see all the time. There is huge background processing used for that, which is the limbic brain.

The prefrontal cortex is rational: 5% to 15%, 10% on average. And 90% is emotional, subconscious. We're not really aware of what's happening, but in the buying decision process, you need to really talk to that part of the brain. If you're going to bet on only 10% when making your marketing, you're bound to fail. That's not a good bet.

## Slide 25: The Focus Star Framework

My framework is the Focus Star, which helps define positioning and clarity across five different attributes. There's no more than five that you can really differentiate on. They all have different impacts on different things.

The five are: **Audience, Purpose, DNA, Personality, and Product.**

They kind of have an order of importance but also not so much. The idea of the Focus Star is a combination. Having only one differentiation can work, and what I'm doing next is finding companies that differentiate with only one of those five, just to go to the extreme example.

My idea of brand positioning and strategy is not to just do one but to try to find the five that make you different. Find the right combination that really helps you find differentiation. Because you might not have a product that is differentiated, which is actually most of the case.

If you really think that you're differentiated thanks to your product, you're bound to fail. If you don't have anything else differentiated than the rational features of your product (price, convenience, speed of execution, customer service), those are a given. Those need to already be there. If that's what you hope your differentiation is going to come from, you're going to fail. Because even if your product is the best in the world, if you don't have anything that makes people connect with you, why would they stay when someone does exactly the same thing cheaper? There's no loyalty. There's no brand attachment. It's harder for marketing to get any kind of brand awareness and get ads to really hook the right people.

You need to find the right combination with this framework.

## Slide 31: The Triangle — Narrowing Your Audience + Halo Effect

This slide is extremely important. *(Florian notes he's always trying to find a better way to explain this.)*

There's a bias: when we see someone being successful and having credibility in one area, we automatically assume they're good at other things. We don't judge them on new things they start if they already have credibility.

**Apple example:** A computer company that started doing music players, then phones. It made sense because they had brand credibility. When Windows came up with a music player, nobody wanted it. Because "it's a computer company, why would you buy a music player from them?" They didn't have the halo effect.

**Celebrity example:** A famous actor shows up and plays three chords on a guitar. Everybody loses their mind, even though for most musicians it's cringe. Halo effect. People assume it's good without questioning the credibility.

**Press conferences:** We ask athletes and actors about geopolitics. We value their opinion on things completely outside their expertise because of the halo effect.

**The triangle analogy for startups:**

- Bottom (widest): "We have drinks to sell." Your market is every person on earth. Why would they care?
- Middle: "We sell very good coffee." Better, but now you compete with every coffee shop, Starbucks, 7-Eleven, Coffee Bean.
- Top (narrowest): "I make coffee for people who like Pokemon." Every Pokemon fan in a 30-mile radius comes, posts on social media, talks about it. You grow fast because of narrow focus.

**Back to Rad Coffee and the halo effect:** Yes, their tribe is emo/goth/punk rock enthusiasts. But once you establish the halo effect, a bunch of people outside that niche are intrigued and want to try it. I go there sometimes and I love it. I'm not emo or goth. Even more interesting, Kevin's friend who is the complete opposite: fashionista, Barbie princess, works in fashion, very classy. She goes there and doesn't even drink coffee, just goes for the sweet drinks. Something about the credibility they've created makes people want to go there.

## Slide 32-35: Focus Star Output — How the five attributes work together in practice

Once you have clarity on the five Focus Star attributes, here's what happens day to day. They inform each other, they work together as a cohesive brand. You don't want to stand out with just one, you want to create an ecosystem that feeds on each other.

**Audience informs everything:**
- Once narrowed down (not just demographics but psychographics: taste, values, hangouts, platforms), it informs the personality/brand identity (logo, colors, shapes designed to attract the audience), product adaptation, content marketing, social media targeting, and ads efficiency.

**DNA/Promise creates consistency:**
- You can check any piece of content: is it conveying the promise/DNA? If someone at Red Bull creates an ad of a guy sitting on his couch working on his computer, that's the opposite of adrenaline culture. They would never do that. It helps create consistency and user experience. Even the hold music when you call customer service should align.
- **Apple example:** When you call Apple customer service, they let you choose the music you want to wait with. No other company does that. Simple, not expensive, but a delightful experience aligned with their DNA of caring about user experience.

**Personality feeds into:**
- Logos, visual and verbal language, colors, typography, photography style, music, user interface.

**Purpose feeds into:**
- Vision and long-term goals. Why do you exist? Why should people care? Why should people join your company? Affects recruitment, HR, internal communication. Pro bono work alignment. Brand partnerships with companies that share the same vision.

**Product feeds into:**
- Innovation, features, benefits, usability, design. Don't think of product just as a widget or service.

**Consistency (maintenance):**
- You can't change all the time. It's scary, but consistency means applying everything everywhere and not pivoting constantly. Website, emails, social media: a cohesive system. You can adapt to different mediums, but the promise must be conveyed, the identity consistent, and the message directed to the right people.
