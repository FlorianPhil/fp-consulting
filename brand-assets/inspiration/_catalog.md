## johnson-gill / social-playing-card-leadership-01.jpg

**Date added:** 2026-03-26
**Source brand:** Lark Creatives (Johnson Gill)
**Format:** Social media post, portrait orientation (approx. 4:5). Playing card concept with B&W photography.

### What catches the eye
- The playing card metaphor is brilliant as a framing device. It instantly communicates a concept ("ace") while giving the layout a recognizable structure that viewers decode in milliseconds.
- B&W portrait photography at massive scale, dominating the center of the frame. The subject IS the card. No separation between concept and person.
- Oversized bold condensed type ("HE PUT EVERYTHING ON THE LINE") placed in the lower third, integrated into the card layout rather than floating on top.
- Single accent color (orange) used for ALL typographic and icon elements against the white card and B&W photo. Extreme discipline: one color does all the work.
- The reflection effect below the photo creates depth and visual sophistication without complexity.
- Secondary copy ("His team had no idea. That's leadership") acts as a punchline, smaller weight, completing the narrative arc.
- The card suit icons (spade, heart) frame the composition top-left and bottom-right, adding structure and playfulness.
- Name and title at the top center serve as the "brand" of the card, clean and minimal.

### What translates to FP.
- **Photography-forward layout:** The subject dominates. This is exactly the direction the FP. social visual system is moving toward (magazine covers, not LinkedIn templates).
- **Single accent color discipline:** Lark uses orange for everything. FP. uses #5A5CF9 for everything. The principle is identical: one bold color, maximum restraint.
- **Oversized type interacting with photography:** Text is part of the composition, not layered on top as an afterthought. The type and image create a unified visual.
- **One idea, massively scaled:** "He put everything on the line" is one thought, blown up. No bullet lists, no multi-point posts.
- **B&W photography + bold color accent:** Already the FP. social direction. This confirms the formula works at scale.
- **Conceptual framing device:** The playing card gives the post an instant hook. FP. could use similar conceptual frames (magazine cover, newspaper front page, book cover, etc.) as structural hooks.
- **Narrative in two lines:** Headline + punchline subtext. Clean storytelling structure.

### What does NOT translate
- **Orange accent color:** FP. is purple-only (#5A5CF9). No warm colors, ever. This is a hard brand rule.
- **The specific playing card metaphor:** While the concept of a framing device translates, directly copying the card layout would read as derivative. FP. needs its own conceptual frames.
- **Condensed typeface:** Lark uses a heavy condensed sans. FP. uses Bricolage Grotesque 800, which is wide and geometric, not condensed. The feeling will differ (wide confidence vs. tall impact).
- **Reflection effect:** Reads slightly gimmicky. FP. would use grain texture or subtle depth instead.
- **Warm energy:** The orange + card game metaphor creates a warm, "player" energy. FP. brand is cooler, more clinical, more "private doctor's office." The adaptation needs to shift the emotional register.

### Tool recommendation
HTML/CSS (the design is structured, geometric, and grid-based. Perfect for precise layout control. Photography would be inserted as a real asset from the FP. library.)

### Adaptation notes
Adapted via Gemini (2026-03-27). Playing card concept reframed as "Brand Therapy diagnostic card." B&W portrait of man in vest/white shirt, purple #5A5CF9 accent replacing orange. Copy: "He built a brand. His competitors built logos." / "That is the difference nobody talks about." Prism icons replace card suits. FP. branding at bottom.

## johnson-gill / poster-oversized-type-dog-01.jpg

**Date added:** 2026-03-27
**Source brand:** Johnson Gill
**Format:** Poster/social media, portrait orientation. Motivational quote with oversized typography and editorial photography.

### What catches the eye
- **Massive slab type dominates 60%+ of the canvas.** "THAT PERIOD IS EVERYTHING" is the entire composition. The type IS the design, not a layer on top of it.
- **Photography sits inside the typography.** The dog between two chairs occupies the negative space within the type block, creating a visual relationship where image and text are structurally interdependent. Not overlaid, not adjacent: integrated.
- **Two-tier message architecture.** Small philosophical setup at the top ("There's a period where nothing seems to be happening") pays off with the massive conclusion below. The scale contrast between the two creates a reading rhythm: whisper, then shout.
- **Single accent color (orange) for ALL type.** Same discipline as the playing card piece. White background, B&W-ish photo, one bold color.
- **The dog metaphor.** A dog sitting patiently between empty chairs. Waiting. Stillness. It's a visual metaphor for the message without being literal or cliched.
- **Extreme whitespace above and below.** The composition breathes. The type doesn't fill the frame edge-to-edge; it floats in generous white space, giving it a premium poster feel.
- **Branding tucked away at the bottom.** "Johnson Gill" in tiny type. The work speaks, the brand whispers.

### What translates to FP.
- **Type-as-architecture:** This is the most direct application of "one idea, massively scaled." The type doesn't support the design, it IS the design. FP. social posts could use Bricolage Grotesque 800 at this scale to create the same poster energy.
- **Photography integrated into type composition:** Instead of photo-with-text-overlay, the image lives within the typographic structure. This creates something more editorial and unexpected.
- **Two-tier message (setup + payoff):** Small philosophical line + massive conclusion. This maps perfectly to FP. content hooks. Setup in Inter 400 (small), payoff in Bricolage 800 (massive).
- **Extreme restraint:** One color, one photo, one idea. No icons, no gradients, no secondary elements. This is the FP. ethos.
- **Poster energy, not social media template energy:** This looks like it belongs on a gallery wall, not a LinkedIn feed. That's the exact target for FP. social content.
- **Brand-as-afterthought branding:** The small credit line approach. FP. could use "FP." or "florianp.com" as a subtle signature rather than a logo stamp.

### What does NOT translate
- **Orange accent color:** FP. is purple-only (#5A5CF9). Non-negotiable.
- **Motivational quote format:** The content itself ("that period is everything") leans inspirational/motivational. FP. content is more diagnostic and strategic. The STRUCTURE works, the genre of message needs to be sharper.
- **Whimsical photography subject:** A dog between chairs is charming but soft. FP. photography features Florian, and the tone is more commanding. The adaptation should use a B&W photo of Florian in a similar "stillness" pose.
- **Rounded, warm feeling:** Despite the bold type, the overall energy is warm and approachable (dog, patience theme, orange). FP. needs to be cooler, more clinical, more "quiet authority."

### Tool recommendation
Gemini (photography integrated into typographic composition requires image generation to composite type and photo as one unified visual. HTML/CSS can't achieve the "photo living inside the type" effect.)

### Adaptation status
- [x] Adapted
- [x] Approved (direction only, not as asset: AI photo not Florian)

### Adaptation notes
Adapted via Gemini (2026-03-27). Type-as-architecture: "CLARITY IS EVERYTHING" at massive scale with B&W seated man integrated into the typographic composition. Two-tier message: "There is a moment where the noise finally stops. That is where the real work begins." (setup) + massive type (payoff). Purple #5A5CF9 only. florianp.com + FP. branding at bottom.

## johnson-gill / poster-harley-tribe-01.png

**Date added:** 2026-03-27
**Source brand:** Johnson Gill / Lark Creatives
**Format:** Poster/social media, portrait orientation. Brand case study with oversized typography and editorial photography.

### What catches the eye
- **Same structural DNA as the other Johnson Gill pieces.** Massive orange slab type dominating the center canvas. B&W photography integrated into the type block. Single accent color discipline. Tiny branding.
- **Brand name AS the design.** "HARLEY DAVIDSON" at massive scale isn't a label, it's the entire composition. The type embodies the brand rather than describing it.
- **Three strategic micro-insights along the top.** "Turn openness into strategic consistency" / "Get bearings, not just feedback" / "Emotion becomes the brand's moat." These are diagnostic, not motivational. Each one is a standalone strategic reframe.
- **Killer punchline structure at bottom.** "You can out-engineer a product. You can't out-engineer a tribe." Setup + inversion. Contrarian framing. This is the sharpest copy in the Johnson Gill collection.
- **Product integrated into typography.** Motorcycle with rider sits within the type block, structurally interdependent with the letterforms. Same technique as the dog and the playing card.
- **Brand-as-case-study format.** Using a well-known brand to teach a strategic principle. The brand is the hook, the insight is the payload.
- **Extreme whitespace.** The composition breathes despite the massive type. Premium poster energy.

### What translates to FP.
- **Type-as-architecture formula.** A core FP. concept ("BRAND THERAPY", "CLARITY", or a sharp phrase) at massive Bricolage Grotesque 800 scale, forming the entire composition. The type doesn't support the design, it IS the design.
- **Photography integrated into type composition.** Subject lives within the typographic block. Structurally unified, not layered.
- **Three strategic micro-insights format.** This is closer to FP. content tone than the dog poster. Diagnostic, strategic, reframe-oriented. FP. could use three Brand Therapy principles as the top line.
- **Setup + inversion punchline.** "You can X. You can't X." This maps directly to FP. hook strategy. Sharp, contrarian, memorable. Example: "You can redesign a logo. You can't redesign what people feel."
- **Brand-as-case-study format.** FP. already analyzes brands in content. This gives a visual format for it: pick a brand, diagnose it, deliver the insight through massive type and a single image.
- **Single accent color discipline.** One bold color does all the work. #5A5CF9 replaces orange.
- **Poster energy over template energy.** Gallery wall, not LinkedIn feed.

### What does NOT translate
- **Orange accent color.** FP. is purple-only (#5A5CF9). Non-negotiable.
- **Showing another brand's product.** Johnson Gill features Harley's motorcycle. FP. wouldn't feature another brand's visual assets. Adaptation would use B&W photography of Florian or a conceptual image.
- **Condensed slab type.** FP. uses Bricolage Grotesque 800 (wide, geometric). The scale and weight translate, but the character width changes the feeling from "tall impact" to "wide confidence."
- **Fan energy.** The Harley piece has a "celebration of a cool brand" energy. FP. needs to be more clinical: "I'm diagnosing why this works" not "isn't this brand cool."

### Tool recommendation
Gemini (photography composited with typography as one unified visual. Same rationale as the dog poster: the photo-inside-type effect requires image generation, not HTML/CSS layout.)
